\opening{Dear Professors Gatheral and Tebaldi,}

I am writing to submit my paper, ``Pricing Prediction Markets: Incomplete Markets, Selection Rules, and Risk Premia,'' for consideration at \textit{Quantitative Finance}.

Prediction market contracts are binary contingent claims whose payoffs are generally not spanned by traded assets. In such incomplete markets, no-arbitrage determines a set of admissible pricing measures rather than a unique price. The paper develops a maintained one-parameter Gaussian selection family within that admissible set. Under a latent Gaussian threshold factor and a variance-preserving exponential tilt of the standardized latent factor, the Wang (2000) transform arises exactly as the pricing-measure selection rule. The paper also develops a broader probit-linear family and a factor-loading interpretation linking the estimated parameter to familiar asset-pricing objects.

Empirically, I estimate the implied selection parameter on 291,309 resolved contracts across six platforms. The estimate is positive on the two traded real-money venues, Polymarket and Kalshi, larger in lower-liquidity and longer-duration markets, and declines over contract life. The play-money platform Manifold yields a negative estimate.

I believe the paper is well suited to \textit{Quantitative Finance} because it treats binary event contracts as an incomplete-market derivatives-pricing problem and combines formal pricing theory with large-sample evidence on market dynamics across platforms. The maintained selection-rule approach and the resulting price-probability wedge should be of direct interest to readers working on derivatives pricing, financial engineering, and market dynamics.

The paper has not been submitted to any other journal. A preprint is available on SSRN:
